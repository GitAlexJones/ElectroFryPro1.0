Equipment & Safety

Backpack Safety Protocol

1. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

All crew must wear non-leaking, non-breathable chest waders, a wading belt to prevent water entry during a fall, and high-voltage lineman's gloves (Class 0, rated to 1000V).

2. Communication ("Power On")

The operator must loudly announce "POWER ON!" before pressing the anode switch. Netters must acknowledge. When releasing, announce "POWER OFF!"

3. Fall Procedures & Immersion Switches

If anyone slips, release the switch instantly. Modern backpacks feature internal tilt-switches that cut power if the operator leans past ~45 degrees. Test these before wading.

4. Cathode Tail Awareness

Ensure the metal cathode tail (the braided wire dragging behind) is fully submerged and trailing freely. It must never wrap around the operator's or netter's legs.

Setting Up the LR-24 — Step by Step
Before You Enter the Water

Step 1 — Measure Water Conductivity
Use a conductivity meter and record the reading. This is the single most important number for choosing your settings — it determines everything else. Record it on the Site tab and in your report.

Conductivity Ranges (PNW Reference):
• Low (<100 µS/cm) — alpine/snowmelt streams, pristine headwaters
• Medium (100–300 µS/cm) — most forested Pacific Northwest streams
• High (300–800+ µS/cm) — agricultural lowlands, tidal influence, urban runoff

Step 2 — Check Water Temperature
Do not electrofish in excessively warm water. High temperatures reduce dissolved oxygen, and electrofishing stress on top of thermal stress can cause significant mortality. Most protocols avoid sampling above 18–20°C for salmonids.

Step 3 — Visual Gear Inspection
Before powering on, check: anode ring is intact and not corroded, cathode braid is undamaged and fully extended, all cable connections are secure, battery is charged, tilt/immersion safety switches are functional. Test the emergency shutdown switch.

Selecting Your Starting Settings

Step 4 — Run LR-24 Quick Setup
The LR-24's Quick Setup auto-selects voltage based on the resistance between your electrodes in the water. It keeps frequency at 30 Hz and duty cycle at 12% — deliberately conservative starting values you'll adjust upward from.

LR-24 Quick Setup Procedure:

1. Power on the LR-24 (rotate the emergency shutdown switch 90° clockwise).
2. Press MENU.
3. Use arrow keys to scroll to Quick Setup.
4. Press ENTER.
5. Make sure the cathode braid is trailing behind you in the water. Submerge the anode ring.
6. Press the anode pole switch briefly — the LR-24 sends a test pulse, measures the energy transfer between electrodes, and auto-selects an appropriate voltage.
7. Quick Setup is complete. Check the status screen to see the selected settings.

Defaults after Quick Setup: Pulsed DC, 30 Hz, 12% duty cycle (4ms pulse width), voltage auto-selected to achieve ~25W average power output.

The LR-24 remembers the last-used settings on power-up. You can also store up to 10 custom setting profiles via STORE SETUP and recall them at different sites with RECALL SETUP.

Step 5 — Manual Settings (If Skipping Quick Setup)

Recommended Starting Points (Juvenile Salmonids):

Voltage:
• Low conductivity (<100 µS/cm): start at 300–500V
• Medium conductivity (100–300 µS/cm): start at 150–250V
• High conductivity (300+ µS/cm): start at 50–150V

Frequency: 30 Hz (safe for scaleless/fine-scaled fish like salmonids and sculpin)

Duty Cycle: 10–15%

Waveform: Pulsed DC (best capture efficiency for most situations)

For heavily scaled fish (bass, carp, minnows), you may need to start higher: 50–60 Hz and 20–30% duty cycle.
In-Water Adjustment — Reading Fish Behavior

Step 6 — Start Fishing and Watch the Fish Response
Begin electrofishing with your starting settings. On the LR-24, press the anode pole switch to energize. Watch carefully how fish react — their behavior tells you exactly what to adjust. Use the LR-24's front panel keys (VOLTS, FREQ, DUTY CYCLE) with the arrow keys to make changes, then press ENTER to confirm each adjustment.

Fish Behavior Decision Tree:

✓ IDEAL: Fish swim toward anode, slow down, and are easily netted
→ Settings are correct. Keep fishing.

Fish TWITCH but DON'T MOVE toward anode
→ Voltage is reaching them but not enough to induce taxis (attraction). Press VOLTS, increase voltage in 50V steps, press ENTER. Stop increasing when fish begin showing a forced twitch response.

Fish TWITCH and then ESCAPE
→ Voltage is adequate — fish feel the field but aren't showing taxis. Press DUTY CYCLE, increase duty cycle by 10%, press ENTER. Repeat until taxis is achieved. If duty cycle reaches max without taxis, reset to 12% and go to frequency.

Fish SWIM AWAY (flee response, no twitching)
→ Field is too weak to stun. Press VOLTS, increase voltage by 50V. If already at max voltage, increase duty cycle by 10%. If still no response, increase frequency by 10 Hz as a last resort.

✖ Fish go RIGID / TETANY (locked up, can't move)
→ Settings are too aggressive. Press FREQ, decrease frequency immediately. Tetany causes severe spinal injuries and vertebral fractures. Net the fish quickly and back off settings. If a fish takes more than 5 seconds to recover, it was shocked too much. More than 15 seconds means settings must be reduced.

✖ No fish response at all
→ Check that the unit is outputting (look at the display). Verify cathode is submerged. Try a significant voltage increase (50–100V). If still nothing, the reach may genuinely have very few fish.
The Adjustment Order Matters

Settings are listed below from highest injury risk to lowest. Always exhaust the safer adjustments before moving to riskier ones:

1. FREQUENCY (highest risk) — Most likely to cause spinal injuries. Never exceed 60 Hz for salmonids. Increase only when voltage and duty cycle changes aren't working.

2. VOLTAGE (moderate risk) — Adjust in small steps (5–20V). Too much voltage in high-conductivity water can overload the unit and injure fish near the anode.

3. DUTY CYCLE (lowest risk) — Safest to adjust. Increasing duty cycle from 10% to 25% puts significantly more energy into the water without spiking the dangerous peak voltage or pulse rate.
Special Cases

High Conductivity / Battery Conservation
In highly conductive water (400+ µS/cm), or when battery life is a concern, you can switch to straight DC (100% duty cycle, no pulsing). This draws less power than pulsed DC at the same capture effect. However, pulsed DC generally has better capture efficiency, so only use DC when conditions demand it.

Between Passes
Keep your settings identical across all passes. If you change voltage or frequency mid-survey, capture probability changes between passes — this violates the core assumption of depletion sampling and invalidates your MLE population estimate.

Reset the Shock Timer Between Passes
On the LR-24: press MENU, arrow to Reset Timer, press ENTER to confirm (any other key cancels). This zeros out the shocking seconds counter so you get a clean per-pass effort reading. Record the shock seconds from each pass before resetting — consistent effort between passes is critical for valid depletion estimates.

Electrofishing Parameters
Conductivity (Cond µS/cm)

Fish internal conductivity is roughly 115 to 150 µS/cm. This dictates your voltage. If water conductivity is very low, it resists electricity; if very high, it conducts better than the fish. Example: Pristine alpine snowmelt streams are highly resistive (20-50 µS/cm). Lowland agricultural rivers or tidal estuaries are highly conductive (400-1000+ µS/cm).

Voltage (V)

Low Conductivity = High Voltage (500V-1000V+) to force current through resistive water. High Conductivity = Low Voltage (50V-200V) to prevent overloading the pack and injuring fish. Example: In a clear mountain creek (30 µS/cm), you may need 700V to create an effective field. In a salty tidal slough (800 µS/cm), 700V will instantly overload the backpack; you must drop to ~100V.

Frequency (Hz)

High frequencies (60+ Hz) induce rapid, rigid muscle contractions (tetany), which increases capture rates but drastically increases the risk of severe spinal injuries. Lower frequencies (15-30 Hz) are safer. Example: Use low frequency (15-30 Hz) when targeting sensitive adult Salmonids or coaxing Catfish from deep cover. Use higher frequency (60 Hz) for small, heavily scaled fish like juvenile minnows that require a faster pulse to stun.

Duty Cycle (%)

Percentage of time electricity is flowing during a pulse cycle. Standard is 10-25%. Higher duty cycle = more total power, but increased risk of muscular hemorrhaging. Example: If fishing at 30 Hz and fish are escaping the electric field, increasing the duty cycle from 10% to 24% will put more overall energy into the water and widen the capture zone without spiking the dangerous peak voltage.

Statistical Methods & Field Protocols
Depletion Theory & Formulas
1. Catchability Coefficient (q)

Formula: q = C / (N × E)
The fundamental concept of depletion surveys. It represents the proportion of the total population (N) caught by one unit of effort (E). Depletion mathematics strictly assume 'q' remains constant across all sweeps.

Why it matters: If you catch exactly 50% of the fish in Pass 1, you must catch exactly 50% of the remaining fish in Pass 2. If fish become "shock-shy" and hide better during the second pass, your 'q' drops, and your final population estimate will be mathematically flawed. This is why effort (shock seconds) must remain identical between passes.
2. Seber-Le Cren (2-Pass Method)

Formula: N = C₁² / (C₁ - C₂)
Calculates the estimated total population (N) when exactly two sweeps are performed.
Mathematical Rule: Catch 1 (C₁) must be strictly greater than Catch 2 (C₂).

Step-by-Step Example:
• Pass 1 (C₁) = 60 fish caught.
• Pass 2 (C₂) = 20 fish caught.

Step 1: Calculate denominator (C₁ - C₂) = 60 - 20 = 40.
Step 2: Calculate numerator (C₁²) = 60 × 60 = 3600.
Step 3: Divide (3600 ÷ 40) = N = 90.

Conclusion: Even though you only caught 80 fish physically, the math estimates the true total population of the reach is 90 fish.
3. Leslie Graphical Method (3+ Passes)

A linear regression method used when you conduct 3 or more passes. Catch Per Unit Effort (CPUE) is plotted on the Y-axis against Cumulative Catch (prior to the current pass) on the X-axis.

Step-by-Step Example:
• Pass 1: Catch = 50. (Cum. Catch before pass = 0). Plot = (0, 50)
• Pass 2: Catch = 30. (Cum. Catch before pass = 50). Plot = (50, 30)
• Pass 3: Catch = 10. (Cum. Catch before pass = 50+30=80). Plot = (80, 10)

Step 1: Plot these three points on a graph.
Step 2: Draw a straight line of best fit through the points descending toward the bottom axis.
Step 3: Find where the line intersects the X-axis (where Catch theoretically hits 0).

Conclusion: If the line crosses the X-axis at 95, your estimated total population is 95 fish.
4. Maximum-Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

The gold standard method for removal-depletion sampling. Unlike the simpler formulas above, MLE finds the population size (N) and capture probability (p) that make your actual observed data most probable.

How MLE Works (Plain English):

Step 1: Assume a starting population N and capture probability p.
Step 2: Ask: "If there really were N fish and we catch p proportion each pass, how likely is the exact catch sequence we observed?"
Step 3: Try different N and p values. The pair that produces the highest likelihood is your Maximum-Likelihood Estimate.

Key advantage over Seber-Le Cren: MLE works with 2, 3, 4, or more passes, produces 95% confidence intervals, standard error, and handles unequal effort between passes. The 95% CI tells you the range within which the true population falls with 95% certainty.

Use the interactive MLE calculator on the Stats tab () to run these calculations in the field.
5. Non-Descending Removal Patterns

A valid depletion survey requires catches to decrease with each successive pass. If Pass 2 catches more fish than Pass 1, the fundamental assumption of constant capture probability is violated.

Common Causes of Non-Descending Patterns:
• Net failure allowing fish to re-enter the reach between passes.
• Inconsistent effort (different shock times between passes).
• Habitat features like log jams releasing fish that were previously sheltered.
• Operator inexperience — the first pass may have poor coverage.

If a non-descending pattern occurs, your population estimate may be unreliable — note it in your report.
Length-Frequency Distribution
What Is It?

A length-frequency histogram groups every fish you caught into size classes (typically 10mm bins) and counts how many fish fall into each bin. This simple chart reveals the age structure of the population — each peak usually represents a different year-class (age group) of fish.

Why It Matters

A healthy stream population should show multiple distinct peaks across the size range, meaning fish of all ages are present — young-of-year, juveniles, and adults. A distribution with only one peak, or with gaps, can signal recruitment failure (no new fish being born), overharvest of large fish, or a recent disturbance event.

Reading a Length-Frequency Chart:

Imagine you caught 40 Rainbow Trout. Your chart shows:
• Peak at 40-69mm — These are young-of-year (age 0+). A strong peak here means good spawning success this year.
• Peak at 110-139mm — These are age 1+ juveniles from last year's spawn.
• Scattered fish at 180-250mm — These are adults (age 2-3+). Fewer fish here is normal because not all juveniles survive to adulthood.

Red Flag Example: If your chart only shows fish in the 150-250mm range with zero fish under 100mm, it likely means spawning failed this year — no juveniles recruited into the population. This is critical information for managers.

The Length-Frequency chart is auto-generated on the Stats tab () and exported to the Excel download as its own sheet with a per-species size class matrix.
Channel Width Transects
What Are Transects?

A transect is a straight-line measurement across the stream channel from one bank to the other. You measure two things at each transect: wetted width (the actual water surface) and bankfull width (the width at the level where water would just start to overflow onto the floodplain).

Why Multiple Transects?

Streams are never uniform — they have pools, riffles, and bends. A single width measurement is misleading. Standard practice is to measure at 3 to 5 evenly spaced transects across the reach and average them. This gives you a representative mean width for calculating reach area.

Calculating Reach Area

Formula: Area (m²) = Reach Length (m) × Average Wetted Width (m)
This area is the denominator for every density calculation — fish per 100m², grams per m², kg per hectare. Without it, you have a population estimate but no way to compare between sites of different sizes.

Step-by-Step Example:

Your reach is 50m long. You measure wetted width at 3 transects:
• T1 (upstream): 3.2m
• T2 (mid-reach): 4.8m (wider pool section)
• T3 (downstream): 3.5m

Step 1: Average the wetted widths → (3.2 + 4.8 + 3.5) ÷ 3 = 3.83m
Step 2: Calculate area → 50m × 3.83m = 191.7 m²

Now if your MLE population estimate is N̂ = 90 fish:
Density = (90 ÷ 191.7) × 100 = 46.9 fish per 100m²

Bankfull width is recorded for habitat characterization but is NOT used in density calculations — only wetted width counts because that's where the fish actually live during sampling.
Per-Species Population & Biomass
Why Per-Species?

The overall MLE lumps all fish together, but species behave differently in an electric field. Sculpin hide under rocks and have low capture probability. Trout are stunned more easily. Running MLE separately for each species produces more accurate population estimates because each species gets its own capture probability (p̂).

Estimated Biomass

Formula: Biomass = N̂ × Average Weight
Biomass is the total estimated weight of a species in the reach. It combines your population estimate with the average weight of fish you actually weighed. This is often more ecologically meaningful than raw count because a reach with 20 large trout may hold more biomass than one with 100 tiny minnows.

Density Metrics

Three standard ways to express density (all require reach area from your width transects):

Worked Example — Chinook Salmon in a 50m × 3.83m reach (191.7 m²):

You estimate N̂ = 25 Chinook with an average weight of 12.5g.

1. Numeric Density (#/100m²)
(25 ÷ 191.7) × 100 = 13.04 fish per 100m²
Best for comparing fish abundance between sites of different sizes.

2. Weight Density (g/m²)
Biomass = 25 × 12.5 = 312.5g
312.5 ÷ 191.7 = 1.63 g/m²
Shows how much fish biomass each square meter of stream supports.

3. Biomass per Hectare (kg/ha)
1.63 g/m² × 10 = 16.3 kg/ha
Standard unit for comparing across watersheds and in published literature. Productive salmonid streams typically range from 20–80 kg/ha.

These values auto-calculate on the Stats tab () when you click "Refresh Estimates" and appear in the Biomass & Density sheet of the Excel export.
Simpson's Diversity Index (D)
What It Measures

Simpson's Diversity Index answers a single question: how evenly distributed is your catch across species? A stream full of one dominant species has low diversity even if hundreds of fish were caught. A stream with many species represented in roughly equal numbers has high diversity.

D is bounded between 0 and 1. Higher values mean greater diversity. It combines two things at once — richness (how many species) and evenness (how balanced their counts are).

The Formula

D = 1 − Σ [ n(n−1) ] / [ N(N−1) ]

Where n is the number of individuals of each species and N is the total number of individuals across all species. The sum runs over every species in your catch.

Step-by-Step Example:
Say you caught 10 Coho, 6 Steelhead, and 4 Cutthroat. Total N = 20.

Step 1: Compute n(n−1) for each species:
• Coho: 10 × 9 = 90
• Steelhead: 6 × 5 = 30
• Cutthroat: 4 × 3 = 12
Step 2: Sum them: 90 + 30 + 12 = 132.
Step 3: Compute N(N−1) = 20 × 19 = 380.
Step 4: Divide and subtract from 1: D = 1 − (132 / 380) = 1 − 0.347 = 0.653

Interpretation: Moderately diverse assemblage — some dominance by Coho, but other species are meaningfully present.
Interpreting D Values

D > 0.70 — High diversity. Multiple species present in balanced numbers. Typical of healthy, undisturbed reference reaches.

D = 0.40 – 0.70 — Moderate diversity. Some dominance but multiple species coexist. Common in most working streams.

D < 0.40 — Low diversity. Community is dominated by one or two tolerant species. Often signals habitat degradation, thermal stress, non-native species pressure, or channelization.

Why Fisheries Biologists Use It

SDI is used in bioassessment programs because it responds predictably to human disturbance. A degraded stream might still have plenty of fish — but usually a handful of tolerant species (e.g., only Sculpin and Dace) that outcompete sensitive species under stress. SDI catches that shift even when total abundance looks healthy.

Important Caveats

SDI is sensitive to sample size. Ten fish in a reach will always give you a less reliable D than 300 fish — because rare species might simply be missed in a small sample. Always report N alongside D.

SDI treats every species equally. Catching three Bull Trout (a threatened species) is weighted the same as catching three Goldfish (an invasive). Use it as one metric among many, not in isolation.

SDI by itself doesn't tell you which species are present — only how evenness is distributed. A site with high D could still be missing sensitive species if it's dominated by tolerant introduced taxa. Always pair SDI with the species list.

Abundance & CPUE
What Is Abundance?

Abundance is simply how many fish are in the reach. There are two ways to express it:

Absolute Abundance — the actual estimated number of fish (your MLE population estimate, N̂). Example: "We estimate 90 Coho Salmon in this reach."

Relative Abundance — a standardized index that lets you compare across sites or years without needing a full population estimate. CPUE is the most common relative abundance metric.

When to use which:

Absolute abundance (MLE) is better when you need a real number for management decisions — harvest limits, endangered species counts, or habitat restoration targets.

Relative abundance (CPUE) is better for trend monitoring — "Are there more fish this year than last year?" You don't need to know the exact count, just whether the index is going up or down.
What Is CPUE?

CPUE = Catch Per Unit Effort
It standardizes your catch by the effort you put in, so you can fairly compare surveys where effort wasn't identical. The "unit of effort" in electrofishing is usually seconds of shocking time, but it can also be per pass, per hour, or per meter of stream.

How to Calculate CPUE

Formula: CPUE = Total Catch ÷ Total Effort

Example 1 — CPUE per Second:

You caught 45 fish over 3 passes totaling 900 shock-seconds.
CPUE = 45 ÷ 900 = 0.05 fish per second

Next year at the same site, you caught 60 fish in 1,200 shock-seconds.
CPUE = 60 ÷ 1,200 = 0.05 fish per second

Even though raw catch went up (45 → 60), CPUE stayed the same — meaning the population didn't actually change, you just fished harder the second year.
Example 2 — CPUE per Pass:

This is the simplest version and what appears on your depletion chart. Each bar on the Y-axis is literally "fish caught on that pass" — which is CPUE where effort = 1 pass.

• Pass 1: 50 fish (CPUE = 50)
• Pass 2: 30 fish (CPUE = 30)
• Pass 3: 10 fish (CPUE = 10)

The declining CPUE across passes is the core evidence that depletion is working — you're removing fish and catching fewer each time.
Example 3 — CPUE per 100m of Stream:

Useful when comparing reaches of different lengths.

Site A: 40 fish caught in a 50m reach → (40 ÷ 50) × 100 = 80 fish/100m
Site B: 60 fish caught in a 120m reach → (60 ÷ 120) × 100 = 50 fish/100m

Even though Site B caught more total fish, Site A has higher CPUE — it's a denser population.
CPUE Assumptions & Pitfalls

CPUE only works as an abundance index if capture probability stays roughly constant. If anything changes the catchability between surveys — different gear, different crew experience, different water clarity, different flow — your CPUE comparison is compromised.

Common CPUE Traps:
• Comparing across gear types — a backpack unit and a boat shocker have totally different capture efficiency. Never compare their CPUE directly.
• Ignoring water conditions — high turbidity dramatically reduces capture probability. A low CPUE during a flood doesn't mean fish disappeared.
• Different operators — an experienced crew catches more per second than a novice crew. Standardize crew experience or note it in your report.
• Hyperstability — fish may aggregate in the remaining good habitat as a population declines, keeping CPUE artificially high even as abundance drops. This is especially dangerous for management decisions.

Always record your exact effort (shock seconds, reach length, crew size, gear model) so CPUE can be properly standardized. The pass timer on the Log tab () helps ensure consistent effort between passes.
Condition Factor & Weight Estimation
Fulton's Condition Factor (K)

Formula: K = (W / L³) × 100,000
Where W = weight in grams and L = length in millimeters. K measures the "plumpness" or overall health of a fish relative to its length. A higher K means the fish is heavier than expected for its size — indicating good feeding conditions.

Step-by-Step Example:
• A Rainbow Trout is 200 mm long and weighs 85 g.

Step 1: Cube the length → 200³ = 8,000,000.
Step 2: Divide weight by cubed length → 85 ÷ 8,000,000 = 0.000010625.
Step 3: Multiply by 100,000 → K = 1.06.

Interpreting K (general salmonid ranges):
• K < 0.80 → Poor condition (underweight, stressed).
• K = 0.90–1.10 → Average condition.
• K > 1.20 → Excellent condition (well-fed, healthy).

K values vary by species and body shape. Elongated fish like Pikeminnow naturally have lower K than deep-bodied fish like Bluegill.
Capture Probability (p̂)

What it means: The estimated proportion of fish in the reach that are caught during a single electrofishing pass. If p̂ = 0.45, approximately 45% of the fish present are captured each sweep.

Why Capture Probability Matters:
• p̂ > 0.50 → Excellent. Your sweeps are highly effective — fewer passes needed.
• p̂ = 0.30–0.50 → Good. Standard for most wadeable streams.
• p̂ < 0.20 → Poor. Deep pools, high turbidity, or poor technique. Your population estimate will have wide confidence intervals, meaning low precision.

Rule of thumb: If capture probability is below 0.20, consider adding more passes to narrow the confidence interval. Use the MLE calculator on the Stats tab () to test how additional passes would affect precision.
Standard Error (SE)

Formula: SE = √(Var(N̂))
Standard Error quantifies the precision of your population estimate. Smaller SE = tighter estimate. The 95% confidence interval is calculated as N̂ ± 1.96 × SE.

Example: If N̂ = 90 and SE = 5, then the 95% CI is 90 ± 9.8, or roughly 80 to 100 fish. That's a tight, reliable estimate. If SE = 25, then CI is 90 ± 49, meaning anywhere from 41 to 139 — too imprecise to be useful.
