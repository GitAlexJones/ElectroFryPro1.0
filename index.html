Backpack Safety Protocol

1. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

All crew must wear non-leaking, non-breathable chest waders, a wading belt to prevent water entry during a fall, and high-voltage lineman's gloves (Class 0, rated to 1000V). Polarized sunglasses are required.

2. Communication ("Power On")

The operator must loudly announce "POWER ON!" before pressing the anode switch. Netters must acknowledge. When releasing, announce "POWER OFF!"

3. Fall Procedures & Immersion Switches

If anyone slips, release the switch instantly. Modern backpacks feature internal tilt-switches that cut power if the operator leans past ~45 degrees. Test these before wading.

4. Cathode Tail Awareness

Ensure the metal cathode tail (the braided wire dragging behind) is fully submerged and trailing freely. It must never wrap around the operator's or netter's legs.

Electrofishing Parameters

Conductivity (Cond µS/cm)

Fish internal conductivity is roughly 115 to 150 µS/cm. This dictates your voltage. If water conductivity is very low, it resists electricity; if very high, it conducts better than the fish. Example: Pristine alpine snowmelt streams are highly resistive (20-50 µS/cm). Lowland agricultural rivers or tidal estuaries are highly conductive (400-1000+ µS/cm).

Voltage (V)

Low Conductivity = High Voltage (500V-1000V+) to force current through resistive water. High Conductivity = Low Voltage (50V-200V) to prevent overloading the pack and injuring fish. Example: In a clear mountain creek (30 µS/cm), you may need 700V to create an effective field. In a salty tidal slough (800 µS/cm), 700V will instantly overload the backpack; you must drop to ~100V.

Frequency (Hz)

High frequencies (60+ Hz) induce rapid, rigid muscle contractions (tetany), which increases capture rates but drastically increases the risk of severe spinal injuries. Lower frequencies (15-30 Hz) are safer. Example: Use low frequency (15-30 Hz) when targeting sensitive adult Salmonids or coaxing Catfish from deep cover. Use higher frequency (60 Hz) for small, heavily scaled fish like juvenile minnows that require a faster pulse to stun.

Duty Cycle (%)

Percentage of time electricity is flowing during a pulse cycle. Standard is 10-25%. Higher duty cycle = more total power, but increased risk of muscular hemorrhaging. Example: If fishing at 30 Hz and fish are escaping the electric field, increasing the duty cycle from 10% to 24% will put more overall energy into the water and widen the capture zone without spiking the dangerous peak voltage.

Depletion Theory & Formulas

1. Catchability Coefficient (q)

Formula: q = C / (N × E)
The fundamental concept of depletion surveys. It represents the proportion of the total population (N) caught by one unit of effort (E). Depletion mathematics strictly assume 'q' remains constant across all sweeps.

Why it matters: If you catch exactly 50% of the fish in Pass 1, you must catch exactly 50% of the remaining fish in Pass 2. If fish become "shock-shy" and hide better during the second pass, your 'q' drops, and your final population estimate will be mathematically flawed. This is why effort (shock seconds) must remain identical between passes.
2. Seber-Le Cren (2-Pass Method)

Formula: N = C₁² / (C₁ - C₂)
Calculates the estimated total population (N) when exactly two sweeps are performed.
Mathematical Rule: Catch 1 (C₁) must be strictly greater than Catch 2 (C₂).

Step-by-Step Example:
• Pass 1 (C₁) = 60 fish caught.
• Pass 2 (C₂) = 20 fish caught.

Step 1: Calculate denominator (C₁ - C₂) ➔ 60 - 20 = 40.
Step 2: Calculate numerator (C₁²) ➔ 60 × 60 = 3600.
Step 3: Divide (3600 ÷ 40) ➔ N = 90.

Conclusion: Even though you only caught 80 fish physically, the math estimates the true total population of the reach is 90 fish.
3. Leslie Graphical Method (3+ Passes)

A linear regression method used when you conduct 3 or more passes. Catch Per Unit Effort (CPUE) is plotted on the Y-axis against Cumulative Catch (prior to the current pass) on the X-axis.

Step-by-Step Example:
• Pass 1: Catch = 50. (Cum. Catch before pass = 0). Plot ➔ (0, 50)
• Pass 2: Catch = 30. (Cum. Catch before pass = 50). Plot ➔ (50, 30)
• Pass 3: Catch = 10. (Cum. Catch before pass = 50+30=80). Plot ➔ (80, 10)

Step 1: Plot these three points on a graph.
Step 2: Draw a straight line of best fit through the points descending toward the bottom axis.
Step 3: Find where the line intersects the X-axis (where Catch theoretically hits 0).

Conclusion: If the line crosses the X-axis at 95, your estimated total population is 95 fish.
